Pride yourself on your machine learning capabilities and evaluation chops?


Here’s your chance to flex those muscles, anon.

Create a machine learning or metrics-based model that produces 15,000 weights indicating the contribution of open-source repositories to the Ethereum universe.


The models that best align with juror rankings for a subset of these dependencies will get to allocate $170,000 to approximately 5,000 open-source repositories—and win prizes worth $50,000 from Vitalik Buterin!


Check out the video below for a quick introduction.


Timeline

This contest is open for about four months, from February 10th to July 25th.


For more details or if you have any questions, join the Telegram group, explore the DeepFunding website, and visit the forum discussion page where you can share your models.


Objective

Given approximately 5,000 projects (represented by their GitHub repositories) and the dependencies among them, your task is to measure the relative contribution of each dependency (source node) to its dependent (target node). You’re free to approach this challenge however you see fit—from feature engineering to model selection and optimization—and you are encouraged to incorporate external datasets as well.

The task is to give edge weights such that A + B + C + D = 1 (Level 1), B vs {B1, B2, B3,B4, B5, B6} (Level 2), B1 + B2 + B3 + B4 + B5 + B6 = 1 (Level 3).


Jurors will conduct random spot checks to answer questions such as:

“Which repo, A or B, has been more valuable to Ethereum’s success?”

“Between B1 and B2, which has contributed more to B?”

“How much of B’s value comes from B itself vs. from its dependencies?”


Submissions receive higher scores if they align more closely with these spot checks. A detailed evaluation method is provided below.


Three Levels of Weights

Your model should assign weights at three levels:

Level 1: Assign weights to the source repositories (seed nodes) for their relative contributions to the target project, Ethereum.

Level 2: Assign a weight to each seed repository relative to its set of dependencies.

Level 3: Assign weights to each dependency of the seed repositories for their relative contributions. This results in approximately 15,000 unique weights between the 30+ seed nodes and ~5,000 child nodes.

In summary, your job is to assign contribution weights to source repositories such that their total contribution to the target repository equals 1. For example: 

Level 1: {web3py, ethereum, 0.05}
Relative to all other dependencies of ethereum, web3py is assigned a weight of 0.05. The weights of all seed repos should sum to 1 with respect to ethereum. 

Level 2: {web3py, originality, 0.6}
The dependencies of web3py collectively amount to 0.4, as 0.6 is attributed to web3py’s own original contribution.

Level 3: {eth-account, web3py, 0.2}
Among all the dependencies of web3py, eth-account is assigned a weight of 0.2. The weights of all web3py dependencies (e.g., eth-account, eth-abi, etc.) should sum to 1.


A note on Level 2


At Level 2, your model predicts how much credit belongs to each of the seed nodes versus its dependencies in growing the Ethereum ecosystem. Here are some examples:

0.2 – The project is largely a fork or wrapper of something else; it does less original work relative to the work in its dependencies.
Examples: Brave (a fork of Chromium), Ollama (a wrapper of llama.cpp).

0.5 – The project is heavily dependent on its dependencies but also has substantial original work.
Example: An Ethereum wallet.

0.8 – The project is mostly original work and depends only on generic libraries; it could likely have been built without those dependencies if necessary.


This level can be visualized as a fraction of credit attributed to the seed project itself vs. that attributed to its dependencies.


Datasets

You may use any external datasets you find helpful for measuring contributions. Below are descriptions about the datasets provided in this contest. You can download them on the Datasets tab.

Human Jury Votes: Model submissions will be tested against a human jury’s votes on which repository contributes more value to the ecosystem.

Ongoing Data Collection: Jury data will be collected on a rolling basis throughout the competition. Some jurors are chosen via a nomination tree, where each juror makes 30 comparisons and nominates two additional jurors. Other jurors are invited based on their expertise with specific seed nodes and dependencies. See the full list of jurors here - https://research.allo.capital/t/join-the-deep-funding-jury/99.

Leaderboard Updates: As new jury data becomes available, the leaderboard will be updated periodically to reflect each model's performance against the latest data.
10 days before the contest ends, we will dismantle the leaderboard and make all public validation data available to participants for training. At that point, their position in the private leaderboard will be what matters in prize distribution.

Data Splits: To prevent "mutual information" from limiting generalizability, jury data is divided into a Training set (publicly available for model training), a Public Test set (for the preliminary leaderboard) and a Private Test set (for the final evaluation).  

For level 1 comparisons between seed nodes, randomization is at the juror level. If a juror makes 30 judgments, they are divided equally between training, private and public test sets.

For level 3 comparisons between child nodes, randomization is at the repo level. For example, eth-account might be in training while eth-abi is in public. In case of a clash, the following notation is followed:

If

repo a - training

repo b - public test

repo c - private test

repo d - training

Then

repo a vs repo d = goes in training

repo a vs repo b = goes in public test, until 10 days before the deadline when it would be transferred to training

repo a vs repo c = goes in private test

repo b vs repo c = goes in public test, until 10 days before the deadline when it would be transferred to private test


Training Data

Pairwise comparisons from jurors are available in the train.csv file. The key columns include:

repo_a, repo_b: The pair of repositories being compared.

parent: The target repository receiving contributions.

choice: Indicates which repository contributes more significantly to the target (1 = repo_a, 2 = repo_b).

multiplier: Specifies how many times more the chosen repository contributes compared to the other.

reasoning: Juror-provided rationale for the assessment (not provided for every comparison).

Evaluation

There are two evaluation measures. The first is primarily indicative, while the second is used to award prizes.


Raw Cost Function


The weights (i.e., project contributions) from your model will be scored based on a cost function that measures how far the weights deviate from juror samples, specifically for questions where jurors have provided comparisons.


Jurors conduct random spot checks by answering questions like:

“Who deserves more credit for the target repo, A or B?”

If the juror picks A, they are asked a follow-up question:

“How many times more credit does A deserve compared to B? (e.g., 3)”


Behind the scenes, each juror comparison is converted into a “logit” form. For example, if a juror says that A contributed 4 times more than B, we can write that as r_A = 4 and r_B = 1. The sample is stored in the form (A, B, ln(r_B) - ln(r_A)), which equals (A, B, -1.386).


If your model assigns w_A = 0.2 and w_B = 0.02, these are first converted to logits:

ln(w_A) = -1.609, ln(w_B) = -3.912.

The term in the cost function is:

[ln(w_B​)−ln(w_A​)−(ln(r_B​)−ln(r_A​))]^2=(−3.912+1.609+1.386)^2=0.840.


If your model assigns w_A = 0.2 and w_B = 0.05, then:

ln(w_B​)=−2.995, ln(w_A​)=−1.609.

So,

[ln(w_B​)−ln(w_A​)−(ln(r_B​)−ln(r_A​))]^2=(−2.995+1.609+1.386)^2=0 (perfect match).

This indicates your model exactly mirrors the juror’s opinion that A contributed 4 times more than B.


The evaluation of the originality score is simpler. If a juror’s originality score for repo A is j_A​, and your model’s assigned originality weight is o_A​, the cost is:

(o_A - j_A)^2.


The final cost is the average of costs over all available juror samples. A lower cost means better model performance.


Contribution to the Composite Model


This measure reflects how much your model influences the ensemble of models that determine the final allocation to projects. Most submissions will likely score 0, as only selected models will form the composite. This approach rewards originality instead of simply copying the leader.


For example, if the second-best model is effectively just a copy of the top model but slightly worse, it may rank second in raw cost function value yet receive a weight of zero here.


You can see an example of the scoring script developed by Vitalik Buterin here.


Two Leaderboards


There are two leaderboards: one for the raw cost function (preliminary leaderboard) and one for the contribution to the composite model (final leaderboard). The jury data will be updated biweekly, and the leaderboards will be updated accordingly. When you submit, your predictions will be immediately evaluated using the raw cost function, and the preliminary leaderboard will be updated. However, the final leaderboard will only be recomputed and updated when new jury data comes in, preventing over-optimization for the preliminary leaderboard.


Prize Distribution


$30,000 will be divided between model submitters based on their contribution to the composite model. For example, if a submission has a score of 0.33, they will get 0.33 out of $30,000.


$20,000 will be distributed between community members that have improved the design of the deep funding competition.


To be eligible for the final prize, please submit a legitimate writeup about your approach at https://ethereum-magicians.org/t/model-submissions-for-ethereum-deep-funding/24200. No writeup, no prize - no matter what your ranking is.


Submission File

The submission format is a CSV file with three columns: repo, parent, and weight.

repo: The dependency repository (or source repo).

parent: The dependent repository (or target repo). For comparisons between a seed repo and its dependencies (as described in Level 2), use originality.
Example: https://github.com/prysmaticlabs/prysm,originality,0.1

weight: The assigned weight to the source repo in the repo column.


A test.csv file is provided with the required repo and parent columns. All you need to do is fill out the weight column. Please include all rows from the original test.csv file—any missing rows will cause an error.


A sample submission file is also included for reference. Do not submit it directly, as its weights are random.


As a fun twist, you can share your submissions on social media as a visualized weighted dependency graph! Just click the “View” button in your Submission tab. (This function will be available later when we have more submissions.)
